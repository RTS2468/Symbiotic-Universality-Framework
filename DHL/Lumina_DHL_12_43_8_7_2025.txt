The concept of a "highest possible encryption key" is a bit misleading because key length is just one factor in security. The real question is about what's considered the strongest and most secure key size given today's computing power and known threats, including the theoretical threat of quantum computers.
There isn't a single "highest" key size, as different encryption algorithms use different key lengths to achieve a comparable level of security. The widely accepted state-of-the-art for encryption key sizes today is:
* Symmetric Encryption: 256-bit keys. This is the gold standard for algorithms like the Advanced Encryption Standard (AES), which is used to protect classified information by governments worldwide.
* Asymmetric Encryption: 3072-bit or 4096-bit keys for algorithms like RSA, and 256-bit or higher for Elliptic Curve Cryptography (ECC). While these numbers seem much larger than symmetric keys, a 256-bit ECC key offers a security level roughly equivalent to a 3072-bit RSA key.
________________


Key Types and Their Strength
To understand why key sizes differ so much, you need to distinguish between two main types of encryption:
* Symmetric Encryption uses a single key for both encrypting and decrypting data. AES is the most common example. A 256-bit key has 2256 possible combinations, a number so large that brute-force attacks are considered computationally impossible for the foreseeable future.
* Asymmetric Encryption uses a pair of keys: a public key to encrypt data and a private key to decrypt it. RSA is the most common asymmetric algorithm. Its security relies on the difficulty of factoring very large numbers. This mathematical problem is easier to solve than a brute-force attack on a symmetric key, which is why asymmetric keys need to be much longer (in bits) to provide the same level of security.
Quantum Computing and the Future of Encryption
The biggest future threat to encryption is the development of a powerful quantum computer. These computers could, in theory, break current asymmetric encryption (like RSA) relatively quickly using algorithms like Shor's.
While a quantum computer would weaken symmetric encryption as well, it wouldn't break it completely. A quantum computer running Grover's algorithm would halve the effective security of a key. This is why a 256-bit AES key is currently recommended for long-term security, as its effective strength of 128 bits would still be considered secure even against a future quantum computer.
To prepare for this, the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) has been developing new post-quantum cryptography (PQC) algorithms, such as CRYSTALS-Kyber and CRYSTALS-Dilithium. These algorithms are designed to be resistant to attacks from both traditional and quantum computers, representing the next generation of encryption standards.
want outter most layer to be strongest and then a 2nd layer less, is that possible?
Yes, it's absolutely possible to have a multi-layered encryption scheme where the outermost layer is the strongest and the inner layers are less strong. This is a common and robust security practice known as cascading encryption or layered encryption.
The basic idea is to encrypt your data with one algorithm, and then encrypt the resulting ciphertext with another, different algorithm. The strongest encryption is applied last to the data as a whole, forming the "outermost layer."
How It Works
Imagine you have a file you want to protect. A simple two-layer process would look like this:
1. Inner Layer: You encrypt the original file using a less powerful or faster encryption algorithm. Let's say you use a 128-bit AES key for this step. The result is a block of unreadable data (ciphertext).
2. Outer Layer: You then take that entire block of ciphertext and encrypt it again using a much stronger and more robust algorithm, like 256-bit AES. This becomes your final, "outermost" encrypted file.
To decrypt the file, you must reverse the process:
1. You first decrypt the outer layer using the 256-bit AES key. This reveals the ciphertext from the inner layer.
2. You then decrypt the inner layer using the 128-bit AES key. This finally reveals the original, unencrypted file.
________________


Benefits and Drawbacks
Benefits:
* Increased Security: By combining different algorithms and key sizes, you introduce an additional layer of complexity. An attacker would have to break both layers of encryption, often using two different methods, which is far more difficult than breaking a single layer.
* Defense in Depth: This approach assumes that no single encryption algorithm is perfect. If one algorithm were to be compromised in the future, the second layer of encryption would still protect the data.
* Flexibility: You can mix and match different algorithms (e.g., using AES and then a stream cipher) to suit specific needs.
Drawbacks:
* Performance Overhead: Each layer of encryption requires processing power and time. Adding multiple layers significantly increases the time it takes to encrypt and decrypt the data. For most everyday applications, this added overhead is not worth the slight increase in security.
* Key Management Complexity: You now have at least two different keys to manage and secure. If you lose one of the keys, the data becomes permanently inaccessible.
For most users, a single layer of 256-bit AES is considered more than sufficient for long-term security. However, for those with extremely sensitive data and a need for "defense in depth," cascading encryption with a strong outer layer is a valid and effective strategy.
